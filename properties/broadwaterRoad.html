02 — The Commissioning Case

Why West Sussex County Council will fill places at this site.

Specialist education is a commissioned market. Places are filled because the local authority directs pupils to them. The evidence below — drawn entirely from WSCCʼs own published material —establishes that WSCC has the motive, the means and the obligation to commission to a new specialist setting on this site.

No. The Evidence (WSCC Published Material) Link What It Means for the Operator
1

WSCC projects a cumulative Dedicated Schools Grant deficit of £413.9m by March 2028. The largest controllable driver is independent non-maintained specialist school (INMSS) spend, currently c.£46m annually at an average c.£64,000 per pupil — against c.£26,000 in maintained settings.

Source: WSCC cabinet letter to government on SEND reforms, July 2025; WSCC written evidence to UK Parliament (FSF 034).

 WSCC has a structural budget problem that can only be solved by changing placement patterns. New in-county provision pitched between maintained cost and INMSS cost saves the council money on every diverted placement. There is pricing headroom for a viable independent operator below the INMSS ceiling.
2

WSCC has previously reported that 61% of pupils with social, emotional and mental health (SEMH) needs were being educated out of county. Home-to-school SEND transport costs have risen 133% since 2018 to c.£28m annually. EHCPs in West Sussex have grown from c.3,400 in 2015 to over 10,000 in 2025.

Source: WSCC Special Free School rationale, 2022; WSCC cabinet member responses, 2024–2025.

 The pupils a new provision would serve are not hypothetical. Hundreds of West Sussex children currently hold EHCPs at distant placements that the council pays for at premium rates plus transport cost. The cohort already exists and is named. Closer in-county provision captures this flow at lower total cost to WSCC.
3

The two DfE special free schools planned for West Sussex were cancelled in late 2025, with capital contribution offered in lieu. Palatine Primary operates at 108% capacity; Oak Grove College (the only maintained 11–19 special school in Worthing) is at functional limit. The county has no maintained secondary SEMH provision in the coastal strip.

Source: DfE correspondence to local authorities, 2025; WSCC SEND Strategy 2026–2030; published school capacity returns.

 The council’s planned new capacity for secondary specialist provision has fallen through. The capital is theoretically available; the site to receive it is not. A site already configured for education use, with planning consent in F1, materially reduces the council’s timeline to closing the sufficiency gap.
4

The Broadwater Road site is centrally located within the West Sussex coastal commissioning strip (c.350,000 residents from Littlehampton to the Adur boundary), with direct A24/A27 access, F1 planning use, c.118,000 sq ft across multiple building typologies, 132 parking spaces, and in-situ vocational workshops suitable for post-16 SEND.

Source: Site particulars; planning records; West Sussex catchment analysis.

 Of the available routes WSCC has to add specialist capacity, this site presents the shortest path: existing education use class, multiple building types supporting phased opening, vocational infrastructure already in place, and a position that minimises transport cost across the strip. It is the obvious physical destination for the capital and the cohort.

The Operator Proposition

WSCC is a commissioner under acute fiscal pressure, with a documented stock of displaced pupils, a cancelled capacity pipeline, and capital available in lieu. This site is the right physical answer to that commissioning problem: educationally configured, centrally located, and immediately available.
